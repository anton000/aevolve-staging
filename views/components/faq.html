What are beta-amyloids?

Beta-amyloid proteins have been extensively researched for their role in Alzheimer’s disease pathology. Beta-amyloid peptides are naturally-occurring in the body and are formed from the cleavage of a larger peptide molecule, Amyloid Precursor Protein (APP). When there is overproduction or insufficient clearing of these peptides, the monomers adhere to each other to form soluble oligomers. Beta-amyloid oligomers are regarded as the “toxic” form of this substance as they have demonstrated a high correlation with Alzheimer’s incidence and the onset of its most prominent symptom, dementia.

How does synthetic nanotechnology extract toxic beta-amyloids from the body?

The newly-launched FDA-approved procedure on offer removes harmful beta-amyloid from the peripheral circulation through a specialized process of hemodialysis. The mechanism involves a synthetic molecular compound, an engineered nanorobot, that mechanically extracts the harmful protein from the blood by binding it with a high specificity, acting as a supermagnet. This process in turn creates a “sink effect”, whereby peripheral extraction re-equilibrates and reduces beta-amyloid in the CNS circulation.

This extensively tested and FDA-approved procedure takes only a few hours to complete. Testing, prescription, admission, therapy, to post-dialysis observation and discharge takes an average of three days.

Can you give me an overview of Alzheimer’s disease?

Every 70 seconds, someone is diagnosed with Alzheimer’s disease. Long before it had a name, its spectrum of devastating neurodegenerative symptoms—primarily characterized by the familiar onset of dementia—has afflicted the human race, exacting a tremendous toll on health, quality of life, productivity, resources, and relationships. Contributing to about 60 to 80% of cases of dementia [1] , Alzheimer’s disease continues to burden more people worldwide as modern populations age. As of 2015, an estimated 46.8 million people all over the world suffered from dementia. The number is expected to reach 131.5 million in 2050. [2]

There is no cure for Alzheimer’s. Current protocols for disease management are palliative or symptomatic at best. Since it was discovered and given a name, Alzheimer’s has been perceived as a death sentence, the diagnosis is a devastating blow to patients and their loved ones. It said that Alzheimer’s is a disease where patients “die twice”—first as they progressively forget themselves and lose autonomy, and second, in a “final act of forgetting”, as one day they simply forget to breathe.
